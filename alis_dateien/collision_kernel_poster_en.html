Aerosol Science & Technology · 45:12 · 1499–1509 (2011) · interactive

One collision kernel for the transition regime, from mean first passage times

Gopalakrishnan & Hogan Jr. · University of Minnesota · Langevin dynamics + dimensional analysis

What it's about

Aerosol particles grow by thermal collisions — particle + particle (coagulation) and particle + vapor molecule (condensation). The collision rate R = β·ni·nj hinges on a single quantity: the collision kernel β. Getting β right is essential for predicting how aerosol size distributions evolve — from new-particle formation in the atmosphere to nanomaterial synthesis.

The collision regimes

Two limits of β are well understood: the continuum regime (large particles) and the free-molecular regime (tiny particles). But real atmospheric aerosols — tens to hundreds of nanometres — sit in the awkward transition regime in between, where both classical models break down. Worse, condensation and coagulation were historically treated with different kernels and different yardsticks.

Research question

Can a single, dimensionless collision kernel describe both condensation and coagulation across the entire transition regime — from a simple, simulation-based approach?

Method

  • Track colliding entities with Langevin dynamics; measure the mean first passage time — the average time until they collide
  • Two setups: a light point mass + a big particle (condensation) and two moving particles (coagulation)
  • Non-dimensionalize (Buckingham-π) → the kernel collapses to one function H of the diffusive Knudsen number Kn_D
  • Recast six published kernels into H(Kn_D) and compare; fit a 4-parameter master curve

Key takeaway

Brownian motion plus dimensional analysis collapses every transition-regime collision rate onto one dimensionless curve H(Kn_D) — so a single kernel covers both condensation and coagulation, accurately enough for almost any dilute aerosol.

Scope & limits: applies to hard-sphere, dilute, Z → ∞ entities with spatially constant friction factors. Electrostatic and van der Waals forces are neglected — important for charged or sub-10 nm particles — but can be added to the Langevin model, at the cost of extra dimensionless numbers beyond Kn_D.
