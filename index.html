Paper 01 — Chen, Wang, Meng & Luo (2021)

Population Balance Equations
Meet Physics-Informed Neural Networks

The Population Balance Equation (PBE) describes how a particle size distribution $n(v, t)$ evolves over time due to aggregation (coalescence) and breakage. Traditional numerical solvers (finite volume, method of moments) are expensive and struggle with complex, data-driven collision kernels. Chen et al. solve this by embedding the PBE as a physics loss inside a neural network.

$$\frac{\partial n(v,t)}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{2}\!\int_0^v \!\beta(v'\!,v\!-\!v')\,n(v')\,n(v\!-\!v')\,dv' - \int_0^\infty \!\beta(v,v')\,n(v)\,n(v')\,dv'$$

How PINNs solve the PBE

  • The NN approximates $n(v, t;\,\theta)$ — the full particle size distribution
  • Physics loss: $\mathcal{L}_\text{PDE} = \left\|\partial_t n - \mathcal{C}[n;\,\beta]\right\|^2$
  • Data loss: $\mathcal{L}_\text{data} = \left\|n - n_\text{meas}\right\|^2$
  • Total: $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_\text{PDE} + \lambda\,\mathcal{L}_\text{data}$
  • Automatic differentiation computes $\partial_t n$ exactly from the network

⚠ The Gap in Paper 1

The collision kernel $\beta$ is assumed to be a simplified, analytical function (constant or purely Brownian/continuum). The PINN framework has no mechanism to incorporate atomic-scale collision data from MD simulations, making it blind to the true physics below 3 nm.

Paper 02 — Gopalakrishnan & Hogan Jr. (2011)

The Theory Bridge:
Transition Regime Collision Kernel

Interactive 02 — Dimensionless Kernel $H(Kn_D)$ — Dahneke Interpolation
$\log_{10}(Kn_D)$: KnD = 1.00
$H(1.00) = 1.285$  ·  Regime: Transition

The diffusive Knudsen number $Kn_D = \bar{c}_\text{rel}\,r_c\,/\,(4\,D_{12})$ quantifies whether a collision is governed by diffusion (small $Kn_D$, large particles) or ballistic motion (large $Kn_D$, nanoclusters). Using Mean First Passage Time theory and Langevin dynamics, Gopalakrishnan & Hogan derive a dimensionless correction factor $H$ that captures the full transition.

$$\beta = 4\pi\,D_{12}\,r_c \cdot H(Kn_D)$$
$$H(Kn_D) = \frac{1}{1 + Kn_D} + \frac{\pi}{4}\,Kn_D$$

Regime Limits

  • Continuum ($Kn_D \to 0$): $H \to 1$ — purely diffusion-controlled (Smoluchowski)
  • Transition ($0.1 \lesssim Kn_D \lesssim 10$): Both mechanisms compete, $H$ has a shallow minimum then rises
  • Free-Molecular ($Kn_D \to \infty$): $H \sim \tfrac{\pi}{4}\,Kn_D$ — ballistic, geometric cross-section dominates

⚠ The Gap in Paper 2

The model assumes hard-sphere particles in a structureless background gas. It ignores van der Waals forces, electrostatic charge interactions, and structural rearrangements of real nanoclusters. Below 3 nm, these effects become dominant and the $H(Kn_D)$ curve deviates significantly from MD-derived values.

Paper 03 — Tamadate, Yang & Hogan Jr. (2022)

The Nano World:
MD-Informed Neural Network for Sub-3 nm Clusters

Below 3 nm, particles are small enough that individual atomic interactions dominate the collision dynamics. Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations integrate Newton's equations for every atom, capturing van der Waals forces, atomic structure, and kinetic energy exchange — but at enormous computational cost. Tamadate et al. train an MLP on thousands of MD collision trajectories to create a fast surrogate model.

MD Simulation Variables

  • Impact parameter $b$: perpendicular offset of the approach trajectory
  • Relative velocity $v_\text{rel}$: initial kinetic energy of the cluster pair
  • Cluster charges $q_1, q_2$: electrostatic contributions to trajectory
  • Temperature $T$: thermal energy affecting sticking probability
  • Outcome: collision probability $P_c(b, v)$ — stick, bounce, or reactive
$$\beta_\text{MD} = 2\pi \int_0^\infty\!\!\int_0^\infty P_c(b,v)\;b\;f(v)\;v\;db\;dv$$

⚠ The Gap in Paper 3

The MLP is a black-box model. It has no physics constraints (not a PINN), is strictly limited to TiO₂ clusters, and provides no generalizability to other materials, size ranges, or temperature conditions not covered by the MD training data. It cannot be embedded into a PBE without additional physics-informed regularization.

Why 3D matters

Real nanoclusters are not perfect spheres. Their faceted atomic structure and non-uniform charge distribution create anisotropic collision cross-sections that cannot be captured by a single scalar impact parameter $b$.

“How can Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) for Population Balance Equations be extended to incorporate atomic-scale collision data from MD simulations in the transition regime, while maintaining physical generalizability across materials?”

Interactive 05 — Proposed PINN Architecture

A neural network learns the collision kernel $\beta(Kn_D, q_1, q_2, T)$ subject to two simultaneous constraints: physical consistency with the PBE, and fidelity to MD simulation data.

Step 1 — Expand MD Data

Run MD simulations for diverse cluster materials (SiO₂, Fe₂O₃, C) and charge states to generate a rich $(b, v, T, q_1, q_2) \to \beta_\text{MD}$ training set beyond TiO₂.

Step 2 — Physics-Constrained Learning

Train a PINN where the neural network predicts $\beta(Kn_D)$ with soft physics constraints: $H \to 1$ as $Kn_D \to 0$ and $H \sim \tfrac{\pi}{4}\,Kn_D$ as $Kn_D \to \infty$.

Step 3 — Embed in PBE Solver

Use $\beta_\text{NN}(Kn_D, q, T)$ as the collision kernel in the Population Balance Equation — enabling physically consistent, data-driven aerosol simulation from nano to macro scale.
