Paper 01 — Chen, Wang, Meng & Luo (2021) · Chemical Engineering Journal

The Continuum Regime:
Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Particle Populations

Research Question

How can we simulate how particles clump together and break apart in industrial processes — efficiently and accurately — even when measurement data is noisy and incomplete?

What they did

They built a Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN). Instead of letting the AI learn freely, they hardwired the Population Balance Equation (PBE) — the conservation law for particle populations — directly into the network’s loss function as a non-negotiable constraint.

What they found

The PINN predicts particle size distributions far more accurately than a plain neural network under noisy conditions. Because it knows the underlying physics, it cannot be fooled by measurement errors — it stays physically consistent even with bad data.

Paper 02 — Gopalakrishnan & Hogan Jr. (2011) · Aerosol Science & Technology

The Transition Regime:
A Random Walk Bridges Two Worlds

Interactive — The Drunkard’s Walk & Mean First Passage Time
Knudsen Kn: 1.00

Low Kn: many tiny chaotic steps — diffusion (continuum). High Kn: fewer large steps — ballistic (free-molecular). The average collision count illustrates the Mean First Passage Time.

Research Question

How do you calculate collision rates in the “in-between” zone — the transition regime — where neither pure fluid mechanics nor pure molecular kinetics alone applies?

What they did

They used Langevin Dynamics — a simulated random walk where the particle is buffeted by gas molecules. They measured how long a particle takes to stumble into a target purely by chance, repeating this thousands of times: the Mean First Passage Time (MFPT).

What they found

All these random collision times collapse onto a single, universal dimensionless curve H(Kn) — valid from the purely diffusive continuum regime all the way to the ballistic free-molecular regime. One curve to rule them all.

Paper 03 — Tamadate, Yang & Hogan Jr. (2022) · Aerosol Science & Technology

The Free-Molecular Regime:
A Neural Network Trained on Atomic Collisions

Research Question

How can we accurately predict collision rates for ultra-small <3 nm TiO₂ nanoparticles, where rigid-sphere models fail because atomic-scale forces like dipole interactions completely dominate?

What they did

They ran thousands of Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations — watching TiO₂ clusters collide atom-by-atom at different speeds and impact angles. They then trained a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) on the results to predict: will they stick or bounce?

What they found

The neural network predicts collision outcomes with >95% accuracy. It captures subtle effects like dipole–dipole attraction that classical models ignore — leading to an effectively larger capture cross-section than pure geometry would predict.

Synthesis — Bringing It All Together

The Gap, The Opportunity
& The Research Question

Paper Core Contribution Scale Key Limitation
Chen et al. (2021) PINN framework for PBE — physically consistent, automatic differentiation through the PDE Macro Kernel $\beta$ is analytical & simplified — no nano-scale data can enter the model
Gopalakrishnan & Hogan (2011) Dimensionless kernel $H(Kn_D)$ — bridges continuum ↔ free-molecular via MFPT + Langevin dynamics Meso Hard-sphere only — ignores van der Waals forces, charges, structural deformations
Tamadate et al. (2022) MLP on MD data — accurate $\beta$ for sub-3 nm TiO₂ where standard theory breaks down Nano Black-box model — TiO₂-specific, no physics constraints, no generalizability

“How can a cross-scale, AI-driven simulation framework be developed that integrates precise microscopic collision data (from MD and Langevin simulations) as physical boundary conditions (via PINNs) into macroscopic population balance models — enabling a unified, real-time prediction of aerosol dynamics across all three regimes?”

Interactive 05 — Proposed PINN Architecture

A neural network learns the collision kernel $\beta(Kn_D, q_1, q_2, T)$ subject to two simultaneous constraints: physical consistency with the PBE, and fidelity to MD simulation data.

Step 1 — Expand MD Data

Run MD simulations for diverse cluster materials (SiO₂, Fe₂O₃, C) and charge states to generate a rich $(b, v, T, q_1, q_2) \to \beta_\text{MD}$ training set beyond TiO₂.

Step 2 — Physics-Constrained Learning

Train a PINN where the neural network predicts $\beta(Kn_D)$ with soft physics constraints: $H \to 1$ as $Kn_D \to 0$ and $H \sim \tfrac{\pi}{4}\,Kn_D$ as $Kn_D \to \infty$.

Step 3 — Embed in PBE Solver

Use $\beta_\text{NN}(Kn_D, q, T)$ as the collision kernel in the Population Balance Equation — enabling physically consistent, data-driven aerosol simulation from nano to macro scale.
